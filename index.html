01
BIO-IDENTICAL LIPIDS

Why tallow is recognized by human skin

Human sebum and bovine tallow share remarkably similar fatty acid profiles — oleic, palmitic, and stearic acids — which is why tallow integrates with the skin barrier without resistance. The skin does not have to work to absorb it.

Lampe MA et al. “Human stratum corneum lipids: characterization and regional variations.” Journal of Lipid Research, 1983. — Fluhr JW et al. “Glycerol and the skin: holistic approach to its origin and functions.” British Journal of Dermatology, 2008.

02
CONJUGATED LINOLEIC ACID

Why we source exclusively from grass-fed animals

Grass-fed tallow contains significantly higher concentrations of CLA than grain-fed sources. Published research has documented CLA as an oil-soluble compound with notable skin-barrier supporting properties. Sourcing matters — it is not a marketing distinction, it is a biochemical one.

Bhattacharya A et al. “Biological effects of conjugated linoleic acids in health and disease.” Journal of Nutritional Biochemistry, 2006. — Lin H et al. “Conjugated linoleic acid and skin barrier function.” International Journal of Molecular Sciences, 2017.

03
FAT-SOLUBLE VITAMINS A, D, E, K

Why delivery method changes everything

Vitamins A, D, E, and K are fat-soluble — meaning they require a lipid vehicle for optimal delivery to skin cells. Water-based products cannot carry them effectively. A tallow base delivers these vitamins in the exact biological medium they were designed to travel through.

Kafi R et al. “Improvement of naturally aged skin with Vitamin A.” Archives of Dermatology, 2007. — Keen MA, Hassan I. “Vitamin E in dermatology.” Indian Dermatology Online Journal, 2016.

04
COLD-PRESSED ROSEHIP SEED OIL

Why we chose rosehip as our primary active carrier oil

Rosa Canina (rosehip) seed oil is one of the most researched botanical oils in dermatology. It is naturally rich in trans-retinoic acid precursors and essential fatty acids. Published clinical research has documented its role in supporting skin appearance and texture over time.

Phetcharat L et al. “The effectiveness of a standardized rose hip powder on skin aging.” Clinical Interventions in Aging, 2015. — Ilyasov IR et al. “Physicochemical properties of rosehip seed oil.” Journal of Pharmacognosy and Phytotherapy, 2016.

05
ROSEMARY CO2 ANTIOXIDANT EXTRACT

Why we use CO2 extraction — not standard essential oil

Rosemary CO2 extract — standardized to minimum 6% carnosic acid — is one of the most effective natural antioxidant systems available for oil-based formulations. It protects the oxidation-prone fatty acids in our formula from going rancid, directly extending shelf life and maintaining active potency. Standard steam-distilled rosemary EO does not deliver the same antioxidant protection.

Oläldele OA et al. “Antioxidant properties of rosemary extract in lipid-based systems.” Food Chemistry, 2014. — Yanishlieva NV, Marinova E. “Stabilization of edible oils with natural antioxidants.” European Journal of Lipid Science and Technology, 2001.
